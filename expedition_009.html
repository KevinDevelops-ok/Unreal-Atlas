Expedition 009

Iceland Highlands

Where Fire Meets Ice

Central Iceland · The Interior Plateau

Reading Time: 14 minutes

Begin
There is a landscape that looks like the beginning of the world, or perhaps the end. A place where the ground itself is still being born, where rivers of molten rock flow beside rivers of ice, where the sky is a theater of light and the earth is a stage of fire. This is Iceland's interior, and it does not feel like a place that belongs to the same planet as the rest of us.

Chapter I

The Interior

Iceland is a young country, geologically speaking. The island itself did not exist 20 million years ago. It has been built, layer by layer, by the eruptions of volcanoes that sit astride the Mid-Atlantic Ridge, the boundary between the North American and Eurasian tectonic plates. The plates are moving apart at a rate of approximately 2.5 centimeters per year, creating a rift that is filled by magma rising from the Earth's mantle. This magma cools and solidifies, adding new rock to the island's surface, expanding its landmass in a process that has been ongoing for millions of years and shows no sign of stopping.

The Highlands, or hálendið in Icelandic, occupy the interior of the island, a vast plateau of volcanic rock, glaciers, and barren desert that covers approximately 40 percent of the country's land area. The region is largely uninhabited, accessible only by a few rough roads that are open only during the summer months. The climate is harsh, with temperatures that rarely rise above 10 degrees Celsius even in July, and winter conditions that make the interior impassable for all but the most determined travelers. It is a landscape that has been shaped by the most powerful forces on Earth: volcanism, glaciation, and the relentless wind that sweeps across the North Atlantic.

The terrain is extraordinarily diverse. In the south, the Highlands are dominated by vast ice caps, including Vatnajökull, the largest glacier in Europe, which covers an area of approximately 8,100 square kilometers and reaches thicknesses of over 1,000 meters. Beneath the ice, volcanoes continue to erupt, their heat melting the glacier from below and creating catastrophic floods known as jökulhlaups, which can release more water than the Amazon River in a matter of hours. These floods have shaped the landscape of the south coast, carving out vast plains of black sand and creating river systems that are among the most powerful in the world.

In the center of the Highlands lies the Sprengisandur, a vast desert of black sand and volcanic gravel that stretches for over 200 kilometers. This is one of the most barren landscapes on Earth, a place where nothing grows and the only signs of life are the tracks of the occasional Arctic fox. The desert was created by volcanic ash and glacial outwash, a combination of fire and ice that has produced a landscape of almost lunar desolation. Driving across the Sprengisandur is an experience of profound isolation, a journey through a world that seems to have been stripped of everything that makes Earth habitable.

And yet, even in this desolation, there is beauty. The black sand desert is not merely black; it is a palette of grays and browns, of volcanic glass and pumice, of rocks that range in size from boulders to dust. The light in the Highlands is extraordinary, changing with the weather in ways that transform the landscape from one moment to the next. A cloud passing overhead can turn the desert from a featureless plain into a landscape of shadow and texture, the low-angle light revealing patterns that are invisible in direct sun. The sky is vast, the horizon distant, and the sense of space is overwhelming.

Chapter II

The Landmannalaugar

If the Sprengisandur is the Highlands at their most desolate, then Landmannalaugar is the Highlands at their most colorful. Located in the Fjallabak Nature Reserve in the southern Highlands, Landmannalaugar is a valley surrounded by mountains that display a range of colors so extraordinary that they seem to have been painted by an artist with an unlimited palette. The mountains are composed of rhyolite, a volcanic rock that is rich in silica and minerals, and which weathers into shades of pink, green, blue, yellow, and orange. The effect is not subtle. It is overwhelming, a riot of color that seems to belong to a different planet entirely.

The rhyolite mountains were formed by volcanic eruptions that occurred approximately 10,000 years ago, at the end of the last ice age. The eruptions were explosive, producing vast quantities of ash and pumice that were deposited around the vent. Over time, the ash consolidated into rock, and the minerals within it oxidized and weathered, producing the colors that make Landmannalaugar famous. The pink and orange hues come from iron oxides. The green comes from chlorite and epidote. The blue and yellow are produced by sulfur compounds and other minerals. Each color tells a story of the chemical processes that have shaped the rock over millennia.

The valley itself is a geothermal area, with hot springs that have been used for bathing since the earliest days of Icelandic settlement. The name Landmannalaugar means "the people's pools," a reference to the natural hot springs that have drawn travelers to this remote valley for centuries. The water emerges from the ground at temperatures of up to 40 degrees Celsius, mixing with cold water from nearby streams to create pools of varying temperatures. Bathing in these pools, surrounded by the colored mountains and the vast expanse of the Highlands, is an experience that borders on the mystical.

The area is also the starting point for the Laugavegur hiking trail, one of the most famous trekking routes in the world. The trail runs for 55 kilometers from Landmannalaugar to Þórsmörk, a valley named after the Norse god Thor, passing through some of the most spectacular landscapes in Iceland. Hikers cross black sand deserts, traverse glaciers, ford rivers, and pass through valleys of green moss and volcanic rock. The trail takes approximately four days to complete, and it offers a journey through the full range of the Highlands' landscapes, from the colorful mountains of Landmannalaugar to the glacier-carved valleys of the south.

Chapter III

The Volcanoes Beneath the Ice

Iceland sits on a hotspot, a plume of magma that rises from deep within the Earth's mantle and feeds the volcanoes that have built the island. There are approximately 130 volcanic mountains in Iceland, of which 30 are currently active. The most famous of these is Eyjafjallajökull, whose 2010 eruption grounded air traffic across Europe and made the volcano a household name. But Eyjafjallajökull is only one of many, and the volcanoes of the Highlands are among the most powerful and unpredictable on Earth.

Beneath the Vatnajökull ice cap lies one of the most active volcanic systems in Iceland: Grímsvötn, a subglacial lake and volcano that erupts approximately every five to ten years. The heat from the volcano melts the ice from below, creating a lake of liquid water that is trapped beneath the glacier. When the lake reaches a critical volume, it bursts through the ice in a jökulhlaup, a glacial outburst flood that can release up to 100,000 cubic meters of water per second. These floods have carved the landscape of the south coast, creating the vast sandur plains that stretch from the glacier to the sea.

The most dramatic eruption in Iceland's recent history was that of Laki in 1783. Laki is not a single volcano, but a fissure system that extends for 27 kilometers across the Highlands. In 1783, a series of eruptions along this fissure released vast quantities of lava, ash, and toxic gases into the atmosphere. The lava flow covered an area of 565 square kilometers, making it the largest lava flow in recorded history. The ash and sulfur dioxide poisoned crops and livestock across Iceland, leading to a famine that killed approximately 20 percent of the population. The effects were felt across Europe, where the volcanic haze caused crop failures and extreme weather that contributed to the social unrest that would eventually lead to the French Revolution.

The Laki eruption is a reminder of the power that lies beneath Iceland's surface. The island is not merely a place of beauty; it is a place of danger, a landscape that can turn from serene to catastrophic in a matter of hours. The volcanoes are not dead. They are sleeping, and they will wake again. The question is not if, but when.

Chapter IV

The Edge of the World

There is a quality to the Icelandic Highlands that is difficult to define. It is not merely the beauty of the landscape, though the beauty is undeniable. It is not merely the scale, though the scale is overwhelming. It is something in the light, something in the silence, something in the sense that you are standing in a place that is still being created, a place where the Earth has not yet finished its work.

The Highlands are a frontier, in the truest sense of the word. They are a place where the human presence is minimal, where the forces of nature operate without constraint, where the landscape changes from year to year as glaciers advance and retreat, as volcanoes erupt and fall silent, as rivers shift their courses and new land emerges from the sea. It is a place that refuses to be tamed, that resists every attempt to make it comfortable or predictable. The roads are rough, the weather is violent, and the distances are vast. To travel in the Highlands is to accept a certain level of risk, to acknowledge that you are not in control, that the landscape is larger and older and more powerful than you will ever be.

And yet, people are drawn to the Highlands. Hikers, photographers, scientists, and adventurers come from around the world to experience this landscape that seems to belong to another planet. They come for the beauty, but they also come for something else: a sense of authenticity, of connection to a world that has not been sanitized or packaged for consumption. The Highlands do not care about Instagram likes or travel blog posts. They simply exist, in all their violent, beautiful, unpredictable glory, and those who visit them are changed by the experience.

The Icelandic people have a word for this: þjóðlegur, which means something like "of the people" or "national character." It refers to the qualities that define Icelandic culture: resilience, independence, a deep connection to the land, and a willingness to accept the hardships that the landscape imposes. The Highlands are the ultimate expression of this character. They are a landscape that demands respect, that tests the limits of human endurance, and that rewards those who meet the test with experiences of profound beauty and meaning.

As climate change accelerates, the Highlands are changing. The glaciers are retreating at an alarming rate, losing mass faster than they can replenish it. The permafrost is thawing, altering the landscape in ways that are not yet fully understood. The weather patterns are shifting, with more frequent storms and unpredictable conditions that make travel in the interior even more challenging. The Highlands of the future will not be the Highlands of today. They will be a different landscape, shaped by different forces, and the experiences that they offer will be different as well.

But for now, the Highlands remain what they have always been: a place of fire and ice, of beauty and danger, of silence and storm. A place where the Earth is still young, still restless, still creating itself before our eyes. And in that ongoing creation, there is a message for all who visit: the world is not finished. It is still being born, still becoming, still revealing new wonders to those who are willing to venture into the unknown.

Final Reflection

The Iceland Highlands are a landscape of contradictions. Fire and ice. Beauty and danger. Creation and destruction. Silence and storm. They are a place where the Earth has not yet decided what it wants to be, a landscape that is still in the process of becoming. And in that unfinished state, there is a kind of hope. The world is not static. It is not a museum piece, preserved behind glass for our admiration. It is alive, dynamic, constantly changing and evolving. The Highlands remind us that we are not the end of the story, but merely a chapter in a book that is still being written. The volcanoes will continue to erupt. The glaciers will continue to flow. The land will continue to rise from the sea. And long after we are gone, the Highlands will still be here, fire meeting ice, creating beauty from chaos, reminding whatever comes next that the Earth is not finished with its work.
